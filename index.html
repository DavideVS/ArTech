Hatsune Miku

Since we are on the theme of music, do you know Hatsune Miku? She is a Japanese singer who travels the world in concert and who stands out for being... 100% digital. For his voice he uses as a basis that of a voice actress and singer, then modified with the help of different software. Real celebrities like you raise - just like Andrew Niccol’s film Simone (see if you are interested in the issues of identity and art through new technologies) - many questions.

There have since been numerous installments, such as additional voice libraries dubbed 'Append', as well as an upgrade for the VOCALOID3 engine, which contained an English vocal release. She received a VOCALOID4 update to her Japanese and English voicebanks in August 2016, as well as a Mandarin Chinese voicebank in September 2017.

Oscar Santillán

(Milagro, Ecuador - 1980) His education began in the early 2000’s as a self-taught artist in his native Ecuador. Those formative times were deeply shaped by working within collective environments. Throughout the years those experiences have remained alive in his practice in the form of collaborations with scientists and other curious minds. In 2011 he obtained an MFA in Sculpture from VCU—Virginia Commonwealth University, in the US; and, consequent fellowships have allowed him to be an artist-in-residence at Skowhegan (US), Jan van Eyck (NL) Ratti Foundation (IT), Delfina Foundation (UK), and Leiden Astronomical Observatory (NL). More recently, he have been one of the igniters setting 'órbitat' in motion, a platform for the encounter of art, science, and technology in Latin America.

JUAN CARLOS LEÓN: LA CAÍDA DEL JAGUAR

The way in which humans have used energy resources has been reflected in global geopolitics throughout history. In Carbon Democracy - Political Power in The Age of Oil (2011), Timothy Mitchell describes the network of fossil fuel relationships through a historical description of energy use. Mitchell points out that, until 200 years ago, the energy used came mainly from renewable sources derived from plants, animals that fed on them, forest wood, water and wind energy, that moved mills and transportation facilities. Most of the world’s population continued to live this way until the middle of the 20th century; however, around 1800, organic resources were gradually replaced by the highly concentrated resources of buried solar energy.
